Given the diagonal size and resolution of a display, the width and height can be calculated as:
Width (W)=S⋅sin⁡(tan⁡−1(HV))Height (L)=S⋅cos⁡(tan⁡−1(HV))\begin{aligned}\textrm{Width}~(W) &= S \cdot \Sin{\Arctan{ \mfrac{H}{V} }} \\[5mm]\textrm{Height}~(L) &= S \cdot \Cos{\Arctan{ \mfrac{H}{V} }}\end{aligned}Width (W)Height (L)​=S⋅sin(tan−1(V​H​​))=S⋅cos(tan−1(V​H​​))​
where:
  • S is the diagonal size of the display panel
  • H is the horizontal resolution of the display
  • V is the vertical resolution of the display
For a SIZE UNIT HRES × VRES display:
L = SIZE × cos(tan-1(HRES/VRES))
Please note that these are the dimensions of the screen only, the viewable image area of the display. It does not include the borders of the casing around the screen.

Note that is also the aspect ratio of the display, so it is valid to use the aspect ratio instead of the resolution. For example, the height of a 16∶9 display, regardless of resolution, is:
L=S⋅cos⁡(tan⁡−1(169))=S⋅cos⁡(tan⁡−1(1.77‾))=S⋅0.490=49.0 % of S\begin{aligned}L &= \rm S \cdot \Cos{\Arctan{ \frac{\raisebox{-0.1em}{$16$}}{\raisebox{-0.1em}{$9$}} }} \\[5mm] &= \rm S \cdot \Cos{\Arctan{ 1.\overline{77} }} \\[5mm] &= \rm S \cdot 0.490 \\[5mm] &= \textrm{49.0~\% of } S\end{aligned}L​=S⋅cos(tan−1(9​16​​))=S⋅cos(tan−1(1.77))=S⋅0.490=49.0 % of S​
However, it is generally safer to use the resolution, because aspect ratios are sometimes subject to rounding or approximation. For example, displays with resolutions of 2560 × 1080 and 3440 × 1440, despite being commonly known as "21∶9" formats, are not actually an exact 21∶9 ratio (and, in fact, are not even the same ratio as each other). Using the aspect ratio instead of resolution would lead to an inaccurate result in this situation. Many online calculators, such as Display Wars, make this mistake with the "21∶9" option in their menus, and encourage people to use aspect ratio instead of resolution.

In most discussions about how to calculate these figures, the solutions tend to start with the Pythagorean theorem and solve from there.[1][2] That's a valid technique, but it's a lot simpler to approach it from the other direction using trig functions.

Computing tan−1(H/V) gives the angle between the side of the display and the diagonal. This angle is opposite from the horizontal edge of the display, and adjacent to the vertical edge of the display. Therefore, the sine and cosine of this angle, which tell us the opposite-over-hypotenuse and adjacent-over-hypotenuse ratios respectively, give us W/S and L/S, and simply multiplying those results by S gives us W and L, the width and height of the display.

Even if you can't remember which one needs sine and which one is cosine, you can simply compute both and sort it out based on which one is bigger; changing a sine to a cosine is very quick on a calculator (much faster than swapping the horizontal and vertical resolution numbers, which is what you need to do with the Pythagorean solution).

But if you just can't stand the sight of trig functions, you can use the Pythagorean approach. This form is more complicated and difficult to remember, and slower to enter into a calculator, but it is favored by many people due to the commonplace allergy to trig functions (and mathematics beyond algebra in general).

Based on the Pythagorean theorem, we know that the diagonal length S is related to the width W and height L by:
S2=W2+L2S^2 = W^2 + L^2S2=W2+L2
S, the diagonal size, is a known value which we can plug in, but we don't know what W and L are. It might appear that we have one equation with two unknowns, an unsolvable scenario. However, since we also know the resolution of the monitor, we know the relationship between W and L even if we don't know their values; W is equal to L times the aspect ratio R, which is equal to H/V. So, the equation becomes:
S2=W2+L2=(L⋅R)2+L2=(L⋅HV)2+L2=L2(HV)2+L2S2=L2((HV)2+1)\begin{aligned}S^2 &= W^2 + L^2\\[5mm] &= \left( L \cdot R \right)^2 + L^2\\[5mm] &= \left( L \cdot \mfrac{H}{V} \right)^2 + L^2\\[5mm] &= L^2 \left( \mfrac{H}{V} \right)^2 + L^2\\[5mm] S^2 &= L^2 \left( \left( \mfrac{H}{V} \right)^2 + 1 \right)\end{aligned}S2S2​=W2+L2=(L⋅R)2+L2=(L⋅V​H​​)2+L2=L2(V​H​​)2+L2=L2((V​H​​)2+1)​
Isolate the desired variable, L:
S2(HV)2+1=L2\mfrac{S^2}{\left( \mfrac{H}{V} \right)^2 + 1} = L^2(V​H​​)2+1​S2​​=L2
Take the square root of both sides:
L=S2(HV)2+1=S(HV)2+1\begin{aligned}L &= \sqrt{\mfrac{S^2}{\left( \frac{H}{V} \right)^2 + 1}} \\[5mm]&= \mfrac{S}{\sqrt{\left( \frac{H}{V} \right)^2 + 1}}\end{aligned}L​=(VH​)2+1​S2​​​=(VH​)2+1​​S​​​
This gives the height of the display, in whatever length units you used for the diagonal measurement. The same formula, but with H and V switched, will give you the width instead (or you can simply multiply the result for height by the aspect ratio (H/V) to get the width more quickly). The trigonometric formulas are a bit simpler in my opinion (no one likes stacked fractions), but everyone has their preferences.
